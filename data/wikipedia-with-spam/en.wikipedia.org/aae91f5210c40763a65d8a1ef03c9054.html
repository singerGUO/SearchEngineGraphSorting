James W. Breyer (born July 1961)[2] is an American venture capitalist, founder and CEO of Breyer Capital, an investment and venture philanthropy firm, and a partner at Accel Partners, a venture capital firm. Breyer has invested in over 40 companies[3] that have gone public or completed a merger,[4] with some of these investments, including Facebook,[5] earning over 100 times cost and many others over 25 times cost.[6] On the Forbes 2016 list of the world's billionaires, he was ranked #722 with a net worth of US$2.4 billion.[1]

Early life and education[edit]

Breyer was born in 1961 in New Haven, Connecticut, the son of Hungarian immigrants.[7][8] His father, John P. Breyer, was an engineer[8] and executive at International Data Group;[9] his mother Eva was an executive at Honeywell.[7] In 1983, Breyer received a B.S. with Distinction in Interdisciplinary Studies from Stanford University.[7][10] He spent his junior year studying in Florence, Italy and is active in what is now Stanford's Breyer Center for Overseas Studies in Florence.[10] He was drawn to Silicon Valley's technology industry and during college he worked part-time for both Hewlett-Packard and Apple Inc.[7] After college, he accepted a job as a management consultant for McKinsey & Company in New York for two years.[11] In 1987, he earned a Master of Business Administration from Harvard University[7] where he was named a Baker Scholar graduating in the top 5% of his class.[12]

Career[edit]

Accel Partners and Breyer Capital[edit]

In 1987 he accepted a job with the venture capital firm Accel Partners in San Francisco and was mentored by founders Arthur Patterson and Jim Swartz.[7] In 1990, he was named a partner and in 1995, he became a managing partner.[10]

Accel Partners was Facebook's biggest shareholder after Mark Zuckerberg, owning an 11% stake at the time of the company's IPO.[13] In 2005, Breyer led Accel Partners' $12.7 million deposit at a $98 million valuation in the then ten-employee startup Facebook.[11] In 2000, Breyer also established Accel-KKR, a joint venture between Accel Partners and the private equity firm KKR.[14] Breyer also led the 2004 management buyout of BBN Technologies from Verizon.[15]

Breyer has led several Series A investments, including Etsy,[16] Clinkle[17] and Circle Internet Financial.[18] He has also led investments in Legendary Pictures[11] and Spotify.[19] Forbes said of Breyer, "He glides easily within and between circles: Silicon Valley, China, Europe, and Hollywood."[11]

In 2006, Breyer founded Breyer Capital, a global equity investor focused on providing capital to help catalyze high impact social and for-profit entrepreneurs.[11] The fund focuses on emerging markets such as China, Brazil, and India.[20] Breyer continues to manage the fund as CEO. Breyer Capital has participated in a number of investments including Harvard's Experiment Fund,[21] Brightcove,[22] Marvel Entertainment[23] and Legendary Pictures.[24]

In 2014 Breyer joined the Wickr board of directors, pledging $30 million in funding. Wickr is a messaging app that seeks to provide encrypted and self-destructing messages for users which pledges a strict commitment to user privacy.[25][26][27]

In July 2016, Breyer also joined the board of the alternative investment firm The Blackstone Group, the largest such firm in the world according to a 2013 New York Times piece.[28][29] [30]

Investment in China[edit]

In 2005, Breyer helped establish a joint venture between Accel Partners and China-based IDG Capital Partners, a pioneering Chinese investment firm behind Baidu and Tencent.[31] Breyer and Patrick Joseph McGovern, the Founder and CEO of IDG, have served as co-leads on the Accel-IDG joint venture's strategic investment committee since its inception in 2005.[32] Breyer is active in the Chinese investment community and continues to invest in the country through Breyer Capital and partnerships with IDG.[33] Additionally, he is a member of the Advisory Board of the Tsinghua University School of Economics and Management, Beijing.[34]

In June 2014 IDG Capital announced the closing of IDG China Venture Capital Fund IV, a $586 million tech venture capital fund focused on making early-stage technology, media and telecom (TMT) investments in China. IDG also announced the participation of Breyer Capital and stated Jim will play a leading strategic advisory role in the new fund.[35][36]

In June 2016, the Wall Street Journal published a piece on what it one of the largest ever China-focused venture capital funds raised, $1 billion in capital commitments for the IDG Capital Fund III of Breyer and IDG Capital.[37]

Professional associations and memberships[edit]

In February 2013, Breyer was elected a fellow of the Harvard Corporation, Harvard University's senior governing board.[10] He also serves on the Board of the Dean's Advisors of the Harvard Business School and is a founding member of the Harvard University Global Advisory Council.[10][38] In December 2005, Breyer was appointed an honorary professor at the Yuelu Academy, Hunan University, China.[39]

He is a member of various World Economic Forum committees,[40] and a member of the Lead Director Network, an organization of directors dedicated to improving the performance of their corporations and earning the trust of their shareholders through more effective board leadership.[41] Additionally, Breyer serves as the Chairman of the Stanford University Engineering Venture Fund and is on the board of the Stanford Technology Ventures Program.[42][43]

Breyer is currently on the boards of Etsy,[16] Legendary Entertainment,[44] Circle Financial,[45] and 21st Century Fox where he is Chairman of the Compensation Committee and a member of the Nominating Committee.[46] He is also a minority owner of the Boston Celtics.[47]

In the past, Breyer has served on a number of public and private boards, including: Facebook from April, 2005 to June, 2013 where he was a Chairman of the Compensation Committee;[48] Wal-Mart Stores, Inc., as the lead/presiding independent Director from 2001 until he resigned in June 2013;[49][50] Marvel Entertainment as founding Chairman of the Strategic Planning Committee from 2006 until their acquisition by The Walt Disney Company in 2009;[51][52] News Corporation from 2011 to 2013;[53] Dell Inc., where he was the founding Chairman of the Strategic Planning and Finance Committee from 2009 until 2013;[54][55] Brightcove, an online video platform which went public in 2012;[56] and Model N as a founding investor and board member. Breyer is a member of the Council on Foreign Relations, Pacific Community Ventures, and TechNet.[10][42] Jim is a Trustee of the Metropolitan Museum of Art, where his wife, Angela Chao, is also a Member of the Chairman's Council, and a Trustee of the San Francisco Museum of Modern Art.[57][58]

Breyer has previously held a number of other leadership positions, including: Chairman of the National Venture Capital Association (NVCA); President of the Western Association of Venture Capitalists; Member of the Board of the Associates of the Harvard Business School; Chairman of the Harvard Business School California Research Center; and Chairman of the Silicon Valley region committee for Stanford University's Campaign for Undergraduate Education.[10][42][59]

Jim Breyer's economic commentary and analysis regarding international market movements and investment trends have been cited in various financial publications, including Fortune, the New York Times and Business Insider.[60][61][62][63]

Awards and achievements[edit]

In August 2010, Fortune named Breyer one of the 10 smartest people in technology, and the smartest investor in technology. For three consecutive years, from 2011–13, Forbes has ranked Jim Breyer number one on the Forbes Midas List of Tech's Top Investors.[64][65][66] Breyer received the Silicon Valley Forum's Visionary Award in 2012.[67] He was also named to the Vanity Fair New Establishment Hall of Fame in 2012.[68] In 2014, Breyer was given a Lifetime Achievement Award by Venture Capital Journal.

Politics[edit]

In April 2013, a lobbying group called FWD.us (aimed at lobbying for immigration reform and improvements to education) was launched, with Jim Breyer listed as one of the founders.[69]

Personal life[edit]

Breyer is married to shipping executive and philanthropist Angela Chao, the youngest sister of Elaine Chao and the widow of Bruce Wasserstein.[70][71] He was previously married to his college sweetheart and impressionist artist Susan Zaroff with whom he had three children,[1][72] and who he divorced in 2004.[73]
